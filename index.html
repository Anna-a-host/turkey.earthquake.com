Earthquake Impact on Turkey and Syria

At its core, an earthquake is a sudden surge of shaking of the Earth's surface caused by the occasional rush of energy from the Earth's crust due to the movement of tectonic plates. This is one of those natural disasters that takes lives of thousands of people. And Turkey was caught in its grip as well.

In its most insidious form, a seismic event of substantial magnitude, measuring 7.8 on the Magnitude Scale, rocked southern and central Turkey and northern and western Syria. Nine hours later, at 1:24 p.m., a second earthquake of magnitude 7.5 about 95 kilometers to the northeast occurred. Then, as if the damage wasn’t bad enough, these unprecedented earthquakes just spiralled into an obliterating cycle of relentless aftershocks which led to mounting losses and exacerbated the situation as their magnitude exceeded 6.0.

The duration of the earthquakes was also not just a fleeting one – the first one lasted approximately 75-90 seconds and the second one – 45-60 seconds.

Consequences of the Disaster

Without a doubt, all these events had unalterable consequences that intervened in the fate of many people and wreaked havoc. The death toll from the earthquake was staggering, exceeding approximately 50,000 individuals, including 45,968 in Turkey and over 5,900 in Syria.

The issue required fostering a multifaceted approach and almost nobody could maintain a laissez-faire attitude, so an array of people felt a compelling urge to help the victims of the tragedy, especially the people who were still under the rubble and needed help.

Economic and Psychological Impact

The economic strain was substantial, with estimated damage reaching 148.8 billion dollars in Turkey and an additional 14.8 billion dollars in Syria. To tackle this issue, tapping into additional funding, financial counselling and intervention by governments of other countries were required.

All of these tragic events made an already seductive allure of studying earthquakes even more attractive. Plenty of new people were entrenched into studying all the facets of the earthquakes because the last one provided an opportunity to study the fault lines in the region of the disaster and the idea of tailoring infrastructure to this sort of events became almost compulsive.
